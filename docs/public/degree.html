Computer Engineering

This degree program is designed to provide students with a strong foundation in the field of computer engineering. It includes a well-rounded curriculum covering both theoretical and practical aspects of the discipline.

Purpose

The purpose of this degree is to equip students with the skills and knowledge required to design, develop, and maintain computer systems, applications, and networks.

Structure

The program is structured into multiple modules, each focusing on key areas of computer engineering. Students will gain hands-on experience and theoretical insights to prepare them for professional careers or further academic pursuits.

Study Plan for Computer Engineering.

First Year

First Cuatrimester

  • Fundamentals of Computer Engineering

    Click on here if you want an in-depth exploration of its content, objectives, and learning outcomes.



  • Administration of Operating Systems I

    The course Administration of Operating Systems I introduces students to the role of the operating system within a computer through the study of its main functions. It presents basic file management techniques and trains students in the installation, basic configuration, and operation of operating systems.

    The course covers an introduction to operating systems, focusing on fundamental elements such as what an operating system is, its functions and objectives, as well as the installation, use, and basic configuration of the most widely used operating systems.



  • Knowledge Management and Person-Centered Soft Skills

    This course on Knowledge Management and Person-Centered Soft Skills is an opportunity for freshmen students to set up the foundations needed to make their university journey something extraordinary.

    The purpose of the course is to deepen the student's knowledge of themselves, recognizing the habits that must be trained in the different dimensions of the person, in order to promote personal maturity. Our aim is for students to discover and personally confront the meaning of their university experience and it's impact in their life, as well as the value of university culture: community search for knowledge, sensitivity towards theory and the deployment of creative actions, comprehensive training and personal and social leadership



  • Introduction to Cybersecurity

    The ultimate goal is for each student to have a basic understanding of the main trends and elements that shape the world of Cybersecurity, both for businesses and end consumers. This will enable them to delve deeper into each of the aspects that make up cybersecurity.



  • Logic and Critical Thinking

    The objective is to develop the ability to reason and think rigorously, helping students maximize their learning, identify as seekers of truth, and prepare to face personal and professional challenges. Additionally, it focuses on shaping leaders capable of bridging data analysis with strategic decision-making, addressing the growing demand for talent in this field.



  • Discrete Mathematics

    The course Discrete Mathematics has as its main objectives the application of set theory and graph theory, the management of finite and modular arithmetic, the use of logical and recursive methods to formalize reasoning systematically, the demonstration of the existence of properties of infinite sets through mathematical induction, and finally, the synthesis of the generation of an infinite number of elements into a finite set of rules in a recursive manner.



  • Mathematics for Engineering I

    This course promotes the practice of analytical, synthetic, reflective, critical, theoretical, and practical reasoning, with its ultimate goal being to discover and model reality. Through serious, rigorous, and passionate work, it leads students toward scientific research.

    The objective of the course is to equip the student with sufficient mathematical skills to tackle common problems in computer engineering, understanding their cross-disciplinary nature and following a process of orientation, representation, decision, and action that leads to finding the solution.



Second Cuatrimester

  • Object-Oriented Programming

    The primary goal of this course is to enable students to master the analysis, design, implementation, and testing of software using the Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) paradigm. Through this comprehensive approach, students will become proficient in utilizing methods, techniques, and tools that are integral to engineering robust and maintainable applications. A special emphasis will be placed on the implementation phase, with a focus on the practical application of concepts through the C# programming as the primary language ensurings that students not only understand OOP principles but also how to effectively apply them in real-world scenarios.



  • Administration of Operating Systems II

    The course covers the introduction to Unix-based operating systems, including their installation and partitioning, shell management, basic/advanced commands, and their objectives.

    The student will learn the basic functioning of a Unix-based operating system, starting with its installation and configuration, and continuing with system administration through shell commands.



  • Electronics and Computer Technology

    The course aims to generate in the student an attitude of amazement and curiosity for the physical reality that underlies the computer systems, promoting an intellectual desire for the seek of knowledge and the habit of wondering about the truth of the things we all relate to. It also seeks to broaden the student's reasoning and lead him/her to question the validity of his/her own beliefs and mental schemes in relation to this physical reality.

    The main objective is learning the fundamentals of electronics and its relationship with the physical operation of computers, peripherals and communication systems used in computing, being able to apply digital logic design techniques for creating simple digital circuits.



  • The Company and Its Processes

    The topics of this subject aim to place the student in the business environment, with special attention to the information processes that take place in any organization. This subject allows the student to mature their abilities of understanding and reasoning of the reality of the ICT role in businesses, through which they will learn to develop and deepen the critical vision of the world around them, the ultimate goal being to achieve true and sensible thinking in a business environment.



  • History of Science and Technology

    The aim is for the student, at the beginning of their studies, to have a panoramic view of the most significant events that have marked the history of their own culture, and particularly, the role that scientific development has played in accelerating modern history. It is also intended that the student situates their profession and thus gains historical awareness of their own field of knowledge, its common roots with philosophy, mathematics, and physics, and its subsequent evolution linked to machine manufacturing, technology, and the construction of systems.



  • Mathematics for Engineering II

    This course promotes the practice of analytical, synthetic, reflective, critical, theoretical, and practical reasoning, with its ultimate goal being to discover and model reality. Through serious, rigorous, and passionate work, it leads students toward scientific research.

    The objective of the course is to provide the student with sufficient mathematical skills to address common problems in computer engineering, understanding their cross-disciplinary nature and following a process of orientation, representation, decision, and action that leads to finding the solution.

Second Year

First Cuatrimester

  • Anthropology and Education for Social Responsibility

    Anthropology and Education for Social Responsibility helps students explore their role in social issues, focusing on understanding human essence through personal and professional lenses. As part of the Integral Training of the Engineer module, it emphasizes reflection on human dignity and the responsibilities of individuals in society. The course combines insights from various anthropological fields and philosophical methods to address human rights, social commitment, and the pursuit of meaningful life.

    The key goals are:

    • Understand personal identity and human dignity to act as agents of change.

    • Tackle global challenges with awareness of corporate social responsibility, solidarity, and human rights.

    • Integrate social and ethical concerns into academic and professional lives.

    The subject aims to foster intellectual and emotional growth, critical analysis of human realities, and the application of technical knowledge to serve humanity. Students are encouraged to work for the common good, today as learners and tomorrow as professionals.



  • Computer Architecture and Organization

    Computer Architecture and Organization provides a comprehensive understanding of the structure and operation of computers, focusing on key components like processors, memory systems, and peripherals. Students gain practical skills in low-level programming (assembly language) and develop the ability to adapt to new languages and environments for processors or microcontrollers

    The Key Topics are:

    • Information representation standards (integers, real numbers).

    • Processor architecture and microarchitecture, instruction sets, and performance parameters.

    • Memory systems, including hierarchical organization and storage technologies.

    • Input/output systems, interconnection buses, and communication techniques.

    • Optimization techniques like caching, pipelining, superscalar/VLIW, multithreading, multicore, and multiprocessor systems.

    Main Goals:

    • Develop software using assembly language.

    • Analyze and evaluate Von Neumann computer systems.

    • Optimize CPU and memory performance using advanced techniques like caching and pipelines. Practical labs focus on assembly programming to solidify concepts.



  • Data Structures and Algorithms

    This course focuses on the study of complexity, iteration and its approaches, as well as data structures and the general techniques for designing them, such as greedy algorithms and dynamic programming. These strategies allow for the systematic solving of large classes of problems. Additionally, data structures like binary trees will be examined. Finally, programming will be carried out using different paradigms, acquiring best practices for building applications in various programming languages, environments, and development tools

    The course aims for students to acquire both theoretical and practical knowledge and develop skills in algorithmic techniques necessary to design efficient applications while fostering critical thinking. It also emphasizes humanistic aspects, with the following goals:

    • Anthropological: Encourage innovative thinking, a search for truth, social interaction, and the development of a service-oriented vocation.

    • Epistemological: Go beyond teaching problem-solving techniques for data structure modeling, emphasizing their principles and ultimate purpose.

    • Ethical: Highlight that modeling and managing structures are not just technical tasks but deeply human activities, aimed at serving humanity and the common good. Special care will be given to aspects such as quality and cost optimization.

    • Purposeful: Promote the construction of models that serve humanity in its environment and contribute to the common good, addressing what data should be included and its purpose in modeling.


  • Analysis and Design of Information Systems

    The course Analysis and Design of Information Systems aims to equip students with methodologies for analyzing and designing applications, with a focus on studying information and its types as a resource in business processes, understood as information systems.

    The course provides a detailed study of system requirements analysis, covering topics such as:

    • Modeling Introduction: Objects, Classes, Relationships, Diagrams, and Fundamental Workflows.

    • UML Modeling: Behavioral modeling, basic and advanced structural models, architectural modeling, and methodologies.

    The course's primary objective is for students to learn methodologies and tools for designing information systems, focusing on analyzing information flow, proper storage, system states, and internal design.



  • Ethical Hacking and Pentesting I

    The objective of this subject is to teach the basic concepts related to Ethical Hacking and one of its main tools, penetration testing (pentesting).

    Specifically, the main blocks of the course wwill be:

    • Installation and configuration of the testing environment.

    • Introduction to Ethical Hacking.

    • Information gathering techniques

    The final objective is for each student to have a basic understanding of the penetration testing process.



  • Forensic Computing

    The objective of this subject is to teach the basic concepts related to forensic computing and the use of some of its main tools.

    Specifically, the main blocks of the course will be:

    • Concealment or anonymity techniques

    • Anonymous browsing.

    • Forensic analysis of systems.

    For this, we will use a Linux distribution specifically created for forensic system analysis, called Caine.



  • Web Development

    The Web Development course teaches methodologies for analysis and development in web environments, focusing on languages, presentation technologies, and information access.

    It covers website architecture, style sheets for accessibility and responsiveness using the Bootstrap framework, and search engine optimization. The course also introduces JavaScript, JQuery, and web services.

    The goal is to equip students with the skills to create a front-end website with proper structure, attractive design, and device adaptability. Specific objectives include mastering HTML5, CSS, Bootstrap, JavaScript, JQuery, and understanding search engine ranking criteria.



Second Cuatrimester

  • Software Engineering I

    Software Engineering focuses on the principles of software development through methods, techniques, and tools that ensure the design and construction of software in an economical, reliable, and effective manner, adhering to current regulations while also considering ethical and anthropological principles. These principles remain constant, unlike regulations, and focus on quality work and service.

    The course covers the basic principles of Software Engineering, emphasizing the "ethics of software" due to the challenges in intellectual property, confidentiality, and appropriate use of technology. It explains the software development process, including the stages of its life cycle: requirements analysis, design, implementation, verification, and maintenance. Special attention is given to the methodologies, techniques, and tools used in each phase.

    The goal is for students to understand software development, integrating people, resources, and intellectual skills to create projects that meet or exceed client expectations, within time, quality, and service constraints. The course aims to foster personal growth by encouraging the development of ethical software, promoting the idea that good work requires dedication, ethical considerations, and focus on improving oneself. It emphasizes the importance of striving for excellence and a comprehensive approach to both professional and personal development.



  • Ethical Hacking and Pentesting II

    The objective of this course is to teach the basic concepts of Ethical Hacking and one of its main tools, penetration testing or pentesting.

    Specifically, the major blocks of the course will be:

    • Information gathering techniques.

    • Attack and intrusion techniques

    The final goal is for each student to have a basic understanding of the main tools that can be used when performing a penetration test in a Linux environment.



  • Secure Development S-SDLC-OWASP

    The main objective of this course is to introduce students to the most common software vulnerabilities according to the classification developed annually by OWASP, and, whenever possible, explain how they can be prevented from the developer's perspective.

    The specific goals of the course is to equip the student with the necessary tools to assess software quality from the perspective of cybersecurity.



  • Statistics

    Through this course, students will learn key concepts such as descriptive statistics, probability, random variables, probability distribution models, and statistical inference, all of which are essential in various engineering fields and critical for interpreting the large volumes of data generated in today's economic and business activities.

    The course aims to teach students how to apply descriptive techniques and analyze information to better understand the world. It covers the basics of descriptive statistics, probability calculation, and statistical distributions. Students will also learn how to estimate population parameters, compare samples, and perform hypothesis tests and linear regression.



  • Operating Systems

    This course covers an introduction to operating systems, including Linux basics and Shell commands. Topics include process description and control, process scheduling algorithms, concurrency, mutual exclusion, synchronization, memory management, paging, segmentation, virtual memory, I/O management, disk scheduling, file management, and the organization of the UNIX file system or directories.

    The main objective is to introduce the nature and characteristics of modern operating systems, focusing on their components, structure, and operational mechanisms.

    • Understand the general structure of a modern operating system.

    • Learn concurrent programming (POSIX).

    • Comprehend virtual memory management.

    • Study the interaction between system hardware and the operating system



  • Databases

    This course focuses on defining the concept, structure, and types of databases. It covers the analysis of problems to identify data and their relationships, enabling the design of conceptual data models that can be transformed into a physical model for a Database Management System (DBMS). The course also delves into the management and use of database data through SQL, and introduces NoSQL databases, highlighting their differences from traditional databases.

    The course aims to provide students with fundamental knowledge in database design, query languages, updates, implementation, and administration. Students will learn to adapt data models to the requirements of a DBMS and understand the different levels of data visibility and their impact on data integrity, availability, and confidentiality within the system. The course also fosters a broader reflection on the human, social, and ethical aspects of technology development.

    The main objectives are:

    • Understand the concepts related to a Database Management System.

    • Differentiate and implement the logical and physical models of a database system.

    • Perform operations in a DBMS using SQL

    • Implement a NoSQL database.

Third Year

First Cuatrimester

  • Software Development and Integration

    The subject of Software Development and Integration focuses on studying different strategies for software integration in the context of Service-Oriented Architecture (SOA). SOA is understood as a software architecture for developing applications that, based on components offering specific services, implement business processes.

    The main objective of this course is to explore various software integration strategies within the framework of Service-Oriented Architecture, which is designed to develop applications by utilizing service-oriented components that implement business processes



  • Ethics and Professional Deontology

    The Ethics and Professional Deontology course establishes general ethical principles and their application in the professional field.

    The course aims to teach ethical principles and values, encouraging students to develop a moral awareness that guides their decisions in personal, social, and professional life, with the goal of improving both their lives and the lives of others.

    The main objectives are:

    • Recognize that human life is moral and must be guided by ethical principles.

    • Develop moral consciousness to make ethical decisions.

    • Understand the relationship between personal and professional life from an ethical perspective.

    • Value moral virtues as the foundation for personal and social transformation.

    • Apply ethical principles in solving problems in everyday and professional life.

    • Foster critical thinking and avoid superficial ethics.

    • Evaluate professional projects from an ethical standpoint, considering their consequences.

    • Integrate theoretical, technical, and ethical knowledge into professional practice.



  • Artificial Intelligence I

    The Artificial Intelligence I course offers an introductory overview of AI, covering both scientific and technical aspects as well as its societal impact. It introduces symbolic models based on formal logic and sub-symbolic models like neural networks, alongside their business applications. The course also encourages reflection on the philosophical and anthropological implications of AI, particularly in relation to human cognitive processes.

    The course aims to provide students with a comprehensive understanding of AI, focusing on both its scientific foundations (strong AI, or general AI) and engineering applications (weak AI). Students will learn how AI replicates human cognitive processes and supports human activities, while also exploring its ethical, legal, and philosophical implications. The course prepares students for advanced AI subjects like Artificial Intelligence II and Knowledge Engineering.

    The main objectives are:

    • Reflect on AI from scientific, anthropological, and ethical perspectives.

    • Understand the science and engineering aspects of AI.

    • Learn techniques for developing intelligent systems and agents, including symbolic and sub-symbolic models, for engineering applications.



  • Human-Computer Interaction

    The Human-Computer Interaction course explores the psychological factors affecting the usability of software and hardware, applying methodological knowledge to the entire life cycle of a graphical user interface (GUI). It focuses on User-Centered Design (UCD) by incorporating techniques that reduce subjectivity in defining systems. The course emphasizes understanding human factors, interaction models, and the ethical responsibilities of engineers in shaping society through design decisions. It encourages students to reflect on their role as engineers and how their work can contribute to a fairer, more user-friendly society.

    The main objective is to apply the necessary methodological knowledge for the life cycle of a graphical user interface, considering human factors and accessibility, and to carry out user-centered design in a real-life context.



  • Mobile and Web Application Security

    The Mobile and Web Application Security course addresses the vulnerabilities that exist at the server and device ends, particularly focusing on the protection of servers that host dynamic content. While transmission channels are encrypted, weaknesses still exist at the endpoints, including the server and mobile devices. This course equips students with knowledge on securing server systems and applications vulnerable to attacks, and introduces mobile device attack vectors, particularly for Android.

    The goal is to introduce students to tools for analyzing vulnerabilities in web servers, web services, and mobile platforms, while teaching basic concepts of web and mobile application security, including the threats and tests that can be developed to assess security.



  • Software Engineering II

    The Software Engineering II course builds upon the knowledge gained in Software Engineering I, focusing on the product concept, its life cycle, and the organizational and collaborative aspects necessary for professional or commercial software development.

    Software Engineering II aims to provide students with a deeper understanding of the complexities of developing software systems, offering a comprehensive perspective that includes the organization of both material and human resources required for their creation and long-term sustainability.

    The main objectives are:

    • Define and manage the software product based on its critical quality attributes.

    • Debunk myths about management methodologies and recognize the true value of agile practices, especially Scrum and DevOps.

    • Transform various types of requirements into a work breakdown structure (WBS), which allows for quantifying costs and delivery times.

    • Understand the evolutionary nature of software and the importance of maintaining its maintainability.

    • Manage product quality using operational metrics and SMART objectives

    • Grasp the purpose and scope of different types of software testing.

    • Reinforce knowledge gained in Software Development and Integration, including branch strategies, continuous integration, deployment environments, and pipelines.

    • Explore different possibilities for software reuse, focusing on popular approaches such as web development frameworks.



  • Computational Complexity

    The Computational Complexity course focuses on different methods for analyzing and designing algorithms, applying them to develop solutions that can ultimately be expressed in a programming language. The course examines the computational complexity of various algorithmic methods, determining the most efficient option for each case.

    The main goal of the Computational Complexity course is to describe the theoretical foundations of the temporal complexity of algorithms. Based on this theoretical knowledge, the course teaches how to calculate the complexity of specific algorithms. The final aim is for students to study the computational complexity of their algorithmic solutions, allowing them to choose the best option among possible alternatives based on various implementation strategies.

    The main objectives are:

    • Understand the theoretical foundations and historical problems of Computational Complexity, such as the "P=NP?" problem.

    • Analyze the complexity of a problem and design the algorithm that solves it with the lowest computational cost.

    • Apply various techniques for computational optimization of algorithms, including Divide and Conquer using recursive algorithms, Greedy Algorithms, Dynamic Programming, Backtracking, etc.

    The course also covers key algorithmic paradigms, applied to basic computational problems like sorting, tree and graph traversal, the knapsack problem, and automatic maze generation, with a special focus on studying the complexity of these problems.



Second Cuatrimester

  • Networks and Distributed Systems

    The Networks and Distributed Systems course describes the various protocols and topologies used in computer networks, allowing students to understand how a network environment functions and design communication solutions that meet specific requirements. It also covers the principles of distributed systems and the main architectures that support them.

    The primary objective of this course is to familiarize students with the existence and functioning of communication networks, as well as their past and future uses, with an emphasis on distributed systems. The course will analyze in detail the main standards on which the TCP/IP architecture is built, both for wired and fiber media (IEEE 802.3 Ethernet) and wireless media (IEEE 802.11 WLAN). It will focus on a practical approach to understanding, designing, and managing communication networks tailored to various scenarios, while considering relevant security aspects.

    Course Content

    • The course begins with the OSI model and general principles of local area networks (LANs).

    • It covers the physical layer in detail, followed by the data link layer with a focus on Ethernet 802.3, interconnecting different networks, and wireless networks (WLAN) based on IEEE 802.11.

    • The network layer is then explored, emphasizing the creation and management of networks and subnets, along with virtual networks (VLANs).

    • The transport layer and key application and security considerations are also addressed.

    • The course concludes with an introduction to the principles of distributed systems.



  • Advanced Databases

    The Advanced Databases course provides students with the necessary tools to add new functionalities to database management systems, improving performance with faster response times and reduced system failure possibilities.

    This course follows foundational database knowledge and focuses on advanced techniques for managing transactions, associating indexes with SQL, working with homogeneous and heterogeneous databases, commitment protocols, query parallelism, and using procedures and triggers. It also covers database administration, security, and techniques for creating data recovery plans.

    The main objectives of the course are:

    • To understand the principles, structure, and fundamental characteristics of distributed database systems, including design, functional, and management aspects.

    • To address the challenges of interconnecting multiple and federated heterogeneous database systems.

    • To learn to select, manage, and operate different database management systems, including handling contingency planning related to backups and data recovery, and assessing the impact of failures.

    • To develop applications using procedural languages for working with databases, including web-based databases.

    • To create applications using modular structures, control structures, exception handling, and process triggers.



  • Statistical Learning and Data Mining

    The Statistical Learning and Data Mining course equips students with both theoretical and practical knowledge to analyze various types of data and extract reliable information through exploration. The course draws on principles from artificial intelligence and statistical learning, and it introduces students to concepts such as data quality and the most common techniques used in statistical learning and data mining.

    The main objective of the course is to teach students the techniques and methodologies for extracting relevant information from different types of data. This will enable them to design solutions that support decision-making processes and perform both descriptive and predictive tasks.

    Specific Aims of the Course:

    • Learn how to explore a dataset to extract the most relevant information using statistical techniques and data visualization methods.

    • Understand and apply predictive classification and regression techniques to datasets.

    • Use descriptive techniques like association analysis and clustering on datasets.

    • Apply a Data Mining methodology, such as CRISP-DM, to knowledge extraction problems.

    • Understand when and how to use different statistical learning and data mining techniques effectively.

    This course prepares students to handle real-world data analysis challenges and develop solutions that assist in decision-making through statistical and computational methods.



  • Artificial Intelligence II

    Artificial Intelligence II builds on knowledge from Artificial Intelligence I, focusing on subsymbolic AI techniques, including neural networks, deep learning, and genetic algorithms. The course is divided into four blocks:

    • Subsymbolic Processing and Machine Learning: Covers the fundamentals of machine learning and the biological inspiration behind neural networks and genetic algorithms.

    • Neural Models: unsupervised (e.g., Self-Associative Maps) and supervised networks (e.g., Perceptron, Multilayer Perceptron), with an introduction to deep learning.

    • Neural Network Applications: Focuses on implementing neural networks, particularly CNNs, and methodologies for building Deep Learning systems.

    • Bio-Inspired Techniques: Explores genetic algorithms for solving complex optimization problems.

    The main objectives are:

    • Understand and apply subsymbolic intelligent systems for pattern recognition, prediction, and optimization.

    • Design and implement neural network applications for industrial and business needs.

    • Study state-of-the-art AI technologies like deep learning and genetic algorithms.



  • Security in the Internet of Things

    This course, part of the Cybersecurity and Ethical Hacking specialization in the Computer Engineering degree, introduces students to IoT devices, common hacking methods, and security techniques.

    The main objectives are:

    • Understand IoT concepts.

    • Gain an overview of threats, attacks, and IoT pentesting

    • Learn the methodology of IoT hacking.

    • Familiarize with IoT hacking tools and security measures.

    • Explore countermeasures for IoT hacking.



  • Blockchain Technology

    Blockchain Technology is a course taught during the second semester of the third year in the Computer Engineering degree (Cybersecurity and Ethical Hacking specialization).

    The course covers:

    • The history and origins of blockchain.

    • Key components and unique characteristics of blockchain.

    • Asymmetric cryptography as a core element of blockchain.

    • Study of public blockchain networks such as Bitcoin and Ethereum.

    The main objectives are:

    • Understand how blockchain works.

    • Identify opportunities for its application.

    • Develop solutions using blockchain technology.



  • Systems Administration

    The System Administration course goal is to delve into the role of the operating system in computers and to train students in the installation, configuration, and administration of the most widely used operating systems.

    Main Content:

    • Historical evolution of operating systems and their impact on hardware and software platform development.

    • Comparative analysis of the properties, capabilities, and characteristics of different operating systems.

    • File management, user administration, and communication between different operating systems.

    • Virtualization infrastructures and services, evaluating on-premise and cloud (Cloud) architectures

    Main Objectives:

    • Understand the role of operating systems in computing platforms.

    • Install and configure operating systems based on the needs of services and available environments.

    • Assess and implement virtualization models and cloud services.

    • Facilitate the integration and communication between different operating systems.

Fourth Year



First Cuatrimester

  • Preparation for Certification I

    The Preparation for Certification I course is designed to prepare students for the initial modules of the CND (Certified Network Defender) certification from EC-Council (International Council of Electronic Commerce Consultants), a U.S.-based organization specializing in cybersecurity certification, education, training, and services.

    The main goal of the course is to equip students with the necessary knowledge and skills to confidently pass the official EC-Council certification exam for Certified Network Defender.



  • Quality Management

    The Quality Management and Software Engineering module provides comprehensive knowledge of quality management principles and their application in software engineering.

    It covers the evolution of quality concepts, current management models, process management, standardization, measurement, indicators, tools, and frameworks.

    The main objective of the module is to integrate quality as a core dimension of software engineering education. Students will learn to identify and apply metrics for creating high-quality software products, manage projects that meet industry standards, and adopt advanced quality management systems effectively.



  • High-Performance Computing

    The High-Performance Computing (HPC) course explores parallel, concurrent, distributed, and real-time programming techniques for multitasking systems. These methods address global challenges like climate change, medical research, AI, and industrial development, emphasizing societal benefit and personal excellence.

    The main objectves are:

    • Address societal problems requiring supercomputing.

    • Learn common supercomputer topologies.

    • Design energy-efficient algorithms for sustainability.

    • Compare supercomputing models like MPI and GPGPU.



  • Security

    The Information Systems are crucial for the daily management of organizations, as the continuity of operations depends on the systems handling information and concentrating the knowledge and expertise of businesses. Therefore, the security of these systems is critical in any design, development, or maintenance process, especially in infrastructures and services associated with information processing and communication technologies.

    The objective of this course is to understand risk analysis and management methodologies as a key aspect of Information Systems Security, analyzing business processes, supporting information assets, and identifying potential threats and vulnerabilities.

    Main Goals:

    • Understand how the evolution of information technologies has made distributed platforms and web environments essential, requiring security considerations from the start of software development.

    • Address software security, focusing on confidentiality, integrity, and availability, considering not only unintentional data loss but also malicious actions and intellectual property rights.

    • Explore the technical, organizational, and legal implications of confidentiality, integrity, and availability in Information Systems Security through case studies.

    • View Information Systems Security as an ongoing process, with technical and professional resources managed throughout planning, design, development, deployment, and maintenance phases.



  • Internship

    The Internship course exposes students to the reality of the business world. During their internship in a real work environment, students can apply and expand the knowledge they have gained throughout their studies. The course encourages students to improve their CV and write a final report detailing their activities in the organization, with ongoing monitoring to assess their learning, performance, and adaptation to the professional world.

    The objective of the course is for students to put into practice the knowledge, skills, and competencies acquired during their university studies in a professional work environment, obtained through a competitive process.

    Specific Goals:

    • Methodically search for a job that allows students to develop the knowledge and competencies gained during the degree program.

    • Understand the specific requirements of the job, plan the necessary effort, and meet the commitments.

  • Information Systems

    This course examines how well-known business information systems have contributed to a company's strategy, from a software engineering perspective, including their design and integration potential. It focuses on integrated systems like ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning), CRM (Customer Relationship Management) applications, workflow systems, and decision-support systems, exploring their functionalities, design requirements, and the underlying architecture. The goal is to strengthen the knowledge of software engineering, enabling students to recognize the solid foundations that certain practices provide to information systems.

    The course aims to solidify the main activities involved in the life cycle of Information Systems (IS) in a company. It addresses the definition, design, implementation, integration, and services related to IS, considering organizational, economic, architectural, and business constraints.     • Develop critical thinking skills regarding information systems in the business world.

    • Learn the technical and organizational complexities of implementing process-oriented information systems, addressing requirements from different areas and decision-making levels.

    • Link information management processes with IT infrastructure and applications, emphasizing their role in business management.

  • Internet of Things

    The course "Programming IoT Solutions" combines both theoretical and practical components. Initially, it covers the current state of infrastructure, technologies, and devices used to create the Internet of Things (IoT), including commonly used protocols, communication methods, networks, and signal acquisition systems in nodes. Later, using microcontroller-based systems and widely used server solutions, students will engage in practical programming exercises for IoT solutions involving data transmission and processing in the cloud.

    The goal is to understand IoT architectures and be able to program IoT solutions based on microcontrollers and connected to cloud services. By the end of the course, students will be familiar with the most common IoT deployment architectures, available networks, frequently used devices, and signal acquisition and communication systems in nodes. They will also be capable of configuring cloud analytics services and programming IoT solutions on the selected platform.

  • The Question of God in the Digital Era

  • Knowledge Engineering

    The Knowledge Engineering course is a branch of Artificial Intelligence (AI) focused on developing Knowledge-Based Systems. It explores problem-solving methods, knowledge extraction in specific areas, and representation using ontologies. The course covers symbolic reasoning techniques, bridging AI with formal systems like logic and mathematics. It emphasizes heuristics for problem-solving and uses state space models to represent domain knowledge, applying search algorithms for concrete cases like video games or route optimization.

    The course aims to study and apply symbolic reasoning techniques in AI, focusing on problem representation and solution acquisition. It specifically explores search methods in state space for problem-solving.

    Specific Aims:

    • Define symbolic AI and its techniques.

    • Present models of Symbolic Processing, particularly State Space.

    • Study and apply search algorithms: uninformed, informed (heuristic), adversarial, and constraint searches to real-world problems.
